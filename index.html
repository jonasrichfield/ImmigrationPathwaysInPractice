Pathway Snapshot

A quick reference guide to common immigration pathways. Remember, only a qualified immigration attorney should determine eligibility.

Family-Based Petition

Relationship-based, but does not automatically create lawful status.

May be relevant when:

Married to or child of a U.S. Citizen or Lawful Permanent Resident.

Refer when:

The abuser threatens to withdraw the petition or controls the paperwork.

VAWA

For certain abused spouses, children, or parents of USC/LPR abusers.

May be relevant when:

Experiencing battery or extreme cruelty by a qualifying abusive relative.

Refer when:

Abuser uses deportation threats, document control, or isolation.

U Visa

For victims of qualifying crimes who suffered harm and were helpful to law enforcement/certifying agencies.

May be relevant when:

Survivor reported DV, sexual assault, felonious assault, or stalking to police/courts.

Refer when:

Survivor wants to cooperate but is terrified of immigration consequences.

T Visa

For certain trafficking survivors.

May be relevant when:

Signs of forced labor, commercial sex exploitation, force, fraud, or coercion.

Refer when:

Someone else controls their documents, wages, housing, or threatens their family.

Asylum

For people afraid to return because of persecution tied to a protected ground.

May be relevant when:

Fleeing domestic violence, gang violence, or LGBTQ+ harm in their home country.

Refer when:

Survivor expresses fear of return or has an upcoming 1-year filing deadline/court date.

SIJ

For certain youth needing juvenile-court protection due to abuse, neglect, abandonment, or similar basis.

May be relevant when:

Child/youth (under 21) is unsafe with one or both parents.

Refer when:

Youth is entering family court, guardianship, or foster care and risks "aging out."

Advocacy / Family Justice / DV & Sexual Assault Services

For Family Justice Center partners, domestic violence advocates, sexual assault advocates, hotline workers, case managers, safety planners, and survivor-support staff.

Why this matters

Immigration status can affect a survivor’s safety, choices, disclosure, housing, employment, access to court, willingness to report, and ability to leave an abusive situation.

Survivors may not say, “I need immigration help.” They may say:

  • “My partner said he will get me deported.”
  • “He has my passport.”
  • “She said I’ll lose my children if I call the police.”
  • “I called the police, but now I’m afraid to keep going.”
  • “I can’t leave because he controls my papers.”
  • “I don’t know what was filed for me.”
  • “My spouse promised to fix my papers, but now uses it against me.”

The goal is not for advocates to determine eligibility. The goal is to recognize when immigration may be part of the safety picture and make a safe, informed referral.

Core principle

Safety first. Immigration screening second. Warm referral always.

Immigration relief can be life-changing, but the first priority is always immediate safety, safe communication, confidentiality, and survivor choice. A legal referral should support the survivor’s safety plan, not pressure them into retelling details before they are ready.

What you may hear from a survivor

Survivors may disclose immigration-related abuse directly or indirectly. Listen for statements about control, threats, documents, fear, reporting, work, children, or dependence.

Relationship & Status Control

  • “My spouse said I have no rights because I’m undocumented.”
  • “He took me to an attorney, but I don’t know what was filed.”
  • “My spouse says if I leave, immigration will come for me.”

Document Control

  • “He keeps my passport.”
  • “She has my birth certificate and my children’s documents.”
  • “My work permit expired, and he would not help me renew it.”

Immigration red flags

These red flags do not prove eligibility. They mean the survivor should be referred for immigration legal screening.

  • Possible VAWA red flags: The abuser is a U.S. citizen or green card holder, controls documents, or uses threats of deportation as coercive control.
  • Possible U visa red flags: Police were called, a report was made, or the survivor spoke with prosecutors/CPS regarding qualifying crimes like DV or sexual assault.
  • Possible T visa red flags: The survivor was forced to work, their wages were withheld, documents taken, or isolated/controlled by another person.
  • Possible Asylum red flags: The survivor says they cannot return home safely due to domestic violence, gang violence, or gender-based harm, and local police did not protect them.
  • Possible SIJ red flags: The survivor is under 21, and a parent abused, neglected, or abandoned them, requiring family court intervention.

What not to promise

  • Do not say, “You definitely have a VAWA case.”
  • Do not say, “You will get a U visa.”
  • Do not say, “If you cooperate with police, immigration will protect you.”
  • Do not tell the survivor to file, travel, move, divorce, or ignore immigration notices without legal advice.
  • Do not contact the abuser to verify facts.

When to refer

Refer immediately if: The survivor has an immigration court date, ICE check-in, ankle monitor, prior removal order, or deadline. The survivor is a youth nearing age-out. The abuser is threatening deportation or holding documents.

Warm referral script

“Some of what you shared may affect your safety and your immigration options. I am not an immigration attorney, so I cannot tell you whether you qualify. But I can help connect you to a qualified immigration legal provider who can screen you safely and confidentially. Before I share anything, let’s talk about what information you feel comfortable sharing and what contact method is safe for you.”

Mental Health / Trauma / Counseling Providers

For trauma therapists, counselors, clinicians, social workers, group facilitators, and behavioral health providers supporting survivors.

Why this matters

Mental health and trauma providers often hear details that survivors may not feel safe sharing with police, courts, immigration attorneys, or case managers at first. A survivor may disclose fear, control, shame, memory gaps, nightmares, document control, or threats of deportation long before they understand that those facts may connect to an immigration legal pathway.

The role of a clinician is not to decide whether someone qualifies for an immigration benefit. The clinical role is to support healing, reduce retraumatization, document professional observations when appropriate, and help survivors connect safely to qualified legal screening.

How trauma affects disclosure

Trauma can affect how, when, and whether a survivor discloses abuse. A survivor may disclose gradually. They may avoid details that feel shameful or emotionally overwhelming. This does not automatically mean the survivor is lying.

Many immigration pathways require survivors to tell painful facts clearly and repeatedly. A trauma-informed approach helps professionals avoid assuming that delayed or fragmented disclosure always equals dishonesty.

Useful clinical observations

Helpful clinical documentation is specific, neutral, and tied to the provider’s role. Useful observations may include:

  • Presenting symptoms (Panic, hypervigilance, dissociation, depression).
  • Functional impact (Impact on parenting, work, sleep, ability to engage with legal systems).
  • Client’s reported fear of abuser, trafficker, or return to country of origin.
  • Client’s reported fear of deportation, court, or government agencies.

How to write support letters without making legal conclusions

A useful clinical support letter should help the legal provider understand the survivor’s trauma, symptoms, treatment, functioning, and barriers to disclosure. It should not try to argue the immigration law.

Sample Language:

“Client reports that her spouse repeatedly threatened to contact immigration authorities if she left the relationship. She reports symptoms including panic, nightmares, difficulty sleeping, and fear of being located by the abuser.”

Avoid:

“This client qualifies for VAWA” or “The client was legally trafficked.”

Trauma-informed referral language

You might say: “You do not have to tell me every detail today. My role is to support you. If immigration status, fear of deportation, or document control is affecting your safety, we can make a referral in a way that protects your privacy.”

Healthcare / Hospitals / Maternal & Child Health

For hospitals, SANE/SART providers, nurses, physicians, social workers, maternal-child health programs, doulas, and healthcare-based violence intervention teams.

Why this matters

Healthcare settings may be one of the first safe places where a survivor discloses abuse, trafficking, reproductive coercion, injury, or immigration-related threats.

Medical providers do not need to determine immigration eligibility. Their role is to recognize safety concerns, provide care, document accurately, use safe communication practices, and connect the patient to qualified survivor services and legal screening.

Healthcare as a first disclosure point

A patient may enter care for physical injury, strangulation, STI testing, pregnancy care, chronic pain, or child neglect concerns. They may not say "I need immigration help." Instead:

  • “My partner won’t let me use birth control.”
  • “I don’t want police involved because I don’t have papers.”
  • “They keep my documents and speak for me.”

Private screening and interpreter safety

Screen privately whenever possible. Do not use the suspected abuser, family member, employer, trafficker, or companion as the interpreter. Ask the accompanying person to step out for a routine part of care.

Normalize the question: “Because violence and stress can affect health, I ask all patients about safety.”

Documentation that can help later

Accurate medical documentation can become important later. Document the patient’s exact words when relevant, objective injuries, strangulation symptoms, mental health presentation, and whether the patient was seen alone using a qualified interpreter.

Documentation Tips:

Better:

“Patient reports that partner keeps her passport and work authorization documents. Patient was interviewed privately using a qualified Spanish interpreter.”

Avoid:

“Patient qualifies for a U visa” or “Patient is lying.”

Before Discharge: Safety, Privacy, and Referral Check

A patient’s discharge plan can either increase safety or unintentionally create risk. Before the patient leaves, pause and ask: “Could anything we are sending home, printing, scheduling, documenting, or communicating create danger for this patient?”

  • Check whether printed materials are safe to take home.
  • Check patient portal and electronic communication risks (does the abuser monitor texts/emails?).
  • Confirm safe contact information and safe times to call.

Courts / Prosecution / Victim-Witness / Justice Partners

For prosecutors, victim-witness staff, court staff, family court partners, and criminal justice partners.

Why this matters

Justice-system partners may not provide immigration legal advice, but their work can strongly affect whether a survivor can later be screened for immigration relief. Court records, police reports, victim-witness contact, and certification practices may all become part of the larger safety and immigration picture.

U visa certification: practical framing

A U visa certification is often misunderstood. It is not a reward. It is not a grant of lawful status. It is a form of documentation from a certifying agency about qualifying criminal activity, victimization, and helpfulness.

Crucial Distinction

Certification confirms agency information.
USCIS decides immigration eligibility.
The survivor still needs legal screening.

What “helpfulness” may look like

Helpfulness may include more than testifying at trial. It may include calling 911, reporting the crime, answering questions, or participating in a forensic exam. A survivor’s fear of continued participation due to immigration threats should trigger safety planning, not automatic assumptions about credibility.

Why early documentation matters

Immigration legal screening often happens months or years after the original incident. By then, survivors may have moved, fled danger, or entered removal proceedings. Useful justice-system documentation preserves the facts that already exist (e.g., incident dates, docket numbers, evidence of intimidation).

Language access & Safe Address

Language access can determine whether a survivor can participate meaningfully. Use qualified interpreters, not children or abusers. Also, justice-system paperwork can sometimes expose safe addresses—always consider confidentiality and safety before recording contact information.

Housing / Community-Based / Violence Intervention Partners

For housing navigators, shelter staff, violence-intervention teams, and community-based organizations.

Why this matters

Survivors often disclose through rent, shelter, food, violence intervention, or family crisis—not by saying “I need immigration relief.” They may say: “I can’t go to shelter because I’m undocumented” or “My landlord is asking for papers.”

Community-based partners are trusted because they are close to where people live. That trust can make them the first safe door.

When immigration vulnerability blocks safety

Immigration issues can make it harder to accept help. A survivor may stay in danger because they lack ID, depend on the abuser for housing or sponsorship, or fear the shelter will call immigration. See immigration vulnerability as part of safety planning, not as a separate issue to deal with later.

Document control as coercion

If a survivor says, “My partner has my passport” or “I owe money and they said I cannot leave until I pay,” this is a major safety and legal red flag. Document control is a profound tool of abuse and trafficking.

What not to promise

  • Do not say that applying for help will never affect immigration.
  • Do not tell someone to ignore immigration mail or miss court.
  • Do not ask for immigration documents unless needed for your role and safe to do so.
  • Do not assume that “undocumented” means “no options.”

Warm referrals from trusted community spaces

A warm referral is safer than a cold handoff. Step 1: Explain the reason for referral. Step 2: Clarify limits. Step 3: Ask consent. Step 4: Confirm safe contact. Step 5: Share only necessary information.

Civil Legal Aid / Family Law / Immigration Referral Partners

For civil legal aid providers, family-law attorneys, LSNJ, VLJ, Partners for Women and Justice, and AFSC-type partners.

Legal issues rarely travel alone

Survivors rarely experience legal problems in separate boxes. A person may come in for a restraining order, divorce, custody, or housing issue—but the facts may also affect VAWA, U visa, T visa, asylum, SIJ, or removal defense.

The role is to spot crossover issues early, avoid harmful advice, preserve useful facts, and refer to qualified immigration legal providers when needed.

Family court facts that may affect immigration

Family-law work may preserve facts that later matter for immigration screening. Watch for: Good-faith marriage facts, abuse timeline, threats of deportation, abuser’s immigration status, abandonment by a parent, and safe address issues. Do not shape the family-law case *only* around immigration, but coordinate carefully.

SIJ Timing & Removal Proceedings

SIJ is time-sensitive. A delay in family court, guardianship, or custody may affect whether the youth can be screened or file before aging out.
For survivors in removal proceedings (immigration court, ICE check-ins), a delayed cross-referral can be devastating. Refer urgently.

Coordination across legal partners

Good coordination can prevent harm. Confirm client consent, clarify who represents the client for which matter, avoid duplicating trauma interviews, and avoid conflicting advice.

Special Warning: Unauthorized Practice

Survivors may be targeted by notarios, fake attorneys, or providers promising fast work permits. Watch for guaranteed results, high fees for vague filings, or advice to ignore court. Encourage clients to seek qualified legal help.

Documentation Tips

Templates and best practices for writing safe, effective support letters.

Purpose of this page

Support letters can help immigration legal providers understand a survivor’s experience, safety concerns, trauma symptoms, service history, and practical barriers.

A strong support letter does not need to sound dramatic. It needs to be accurate, careful, trauma-informed, and limited to the writer’s role. This page is designed for advocates, case managers, clinicians, healthcare providers, legal-services partners, community organizations, and other professionals who may be asked to write a letter or document survivor-related information.

Core Rule

Document what you know.
Do not decide immigration eligibility.

A support letter should not say:

  • “This client qualifies for VAWA.”
  • “This is definitely a U visa case.”
  • “This person was legally trafficked.”
  • “This client has a strong asylum claim.”
  • “This child qualifies for SIJ.”

Instead, a support letter may say:

  • “The client reported…”
  • “I observed…”
  • “Our agency provided…”
  • “The client presented with…”
  • “The client disclosed/requested…”

That distinction protects the survivor, the writer, and the legal provider.

What a support letter can do

A support letter may help document:

  • The relationship between the survivor and the provider.
  • The services provided.
  • The survivor’s reported experiences.
  • Observed emotional or physical impact.
  • Safety concerns & trauma symptoms.
  • Barriers to disclosure.
  • Language-access needs.
  • Safe contact concerns.
  • Document control.
  • Threats of deportation.
  • Fear of reporting or court.
  • Fear of returning to country of origin.
  • Housing instability.
  • Medical concerns.
  • Child safety concerns.
  • Trafficking indicators.
  • Referrals made.

The letter should stay within the writer’s professional role.

What a support letter should not do

A support letter should not:

  • Give legal advice or promise immigration results.
  • Use legal labels outside the writer’s role.
  • Exaggerate facts or add facts the survivor did not report.
  • Guess about immigration eligibility.
  • Diagnose unless the writer is qualified to diagnose.
  • State that the survivor is “credible” as a legal conclusion.
  • Argue the whole immigration case.
  • Include unnecessary graphic details.
  • Share unsafe addresses or reveal children’s schools or shelter locations unnecessarily.
  • Attach therapy notes or medical records unless specifically requested, consented to, and safely transmitted.
  • Pressure a survivor to disclose more than they are ready to share.

Before writing a letter

Before writing, confirm:

  • The survivor requested the letter or consented to it.
  • The survivor understands who will receive it and what information will be included.
  • The survivor has reviewed the letter when possible.
  • The letter will not include unsafe addresses or contact information.
  • The letter will not include unnecessary trauma details.
  • The letter is being sent through a safe method.
  • The writer understands their role and limits, and is not making legal conclusions.

If there is any doubt, coordinate with the immigration legal provider before sending the letter.

Safe-contact warning

Support letters can create danger if they include unsafe contact information. Before including address, phone, email, workplace, school, shelter, or family information, ask:

  • Is this address/phone number/email safe? Can mail be received safely?
  • Does the abuser monitor the survivor’s phone, email, location, patient portal, mail, or social media?
  • Could the letter be seen by the abuser? Could it reveal a shelter, school, workplace, or new residence?

When in doubt, leave out unsafe contact details and coordinate with the legal provider.

Recommended support letter structure

  1. Writer identification: State your name, title, agency, role, and relationship to the survivor.
    Example: “My name is [Name], and I am a [Title] at [Organization]. I have provided [services] to [Client Name] since approximately [date].”
  2. Purpose of the letter: State why the letter is being written.
    Example: “This letter is provided at the client’s request... I am not providing a legal opinion...”
  3. Service history: Describe the services provided.
    Example: “Our agency has provided safety planning, crisis support...”
  4. Reported history: Describe what the survivor reported, using careful language.
    Example: “The client reported that her partner threatened to contact immigration authorities...”
  5. Observations: Describe what you personally observed.
    Example: “During meetings, the client appeared tearful and anxious...”
  6. Impact: Explain how the reported experience affected the survivor’s functioning or safety.
    Example: “The client reported that these threats made her afraid to contact police...”
  7. Referrals and support provided: Describe what your agency did.
  8. Limits: End with a clear boundary.
    Example: “This letter does not offer a legal conclusion...”

Language Guidance

Language that helps

  • “Client reported/disclosed/described/stated…”
  • “I observed…”
  • “Our agency provided…”
  • “The client appeared/requested/was referred…”
  • “The client reported fear of/difficulty…”
  • “The client reported that the abuser threatened…”
  • “The client reported that another person controlled…”
  • “Based on my role, I observed…”
  • “In my clinical opinion / Within my professional role…”
  • “This letter is not a legal opinion…”

Language to avoid

  • “Client qualifies for / deserves…”
  • “Client is credible / definitely telling the truth.”
  • “Client has a strong case / This proves…”
  • “This is clearly trafficking / persecution / extreme cruelty.”
  • “USCIS should approve / The judge should grant…”
  • “The client will get immigration status.”
  • “The client refused / failed to leave / chose to stay…”
  • “The client did not cooperate…”

These phrases can be harmful, judgmental, legally inaccurate, or outside the writer’s role.

Stronger alternatives

“Client refused to cooperate.”

Write: “Client reported fear of participating because the abuser threatened retaliation and deportation.”

“Client failed to leave the abuser.”

Write: “Client reported that leaving was difficult because the abuser controlled housing, money, documents, and transportation.”

“Client has a strong VAWA case.”

Write: “Client reported abuse by a spouse who she believes is a lawful permanent resident. The client was referred to immigration legal services for screening.”

“Client is credible.”

Write: “Client’s reports have remained generally consistent during our work together,” or “Client reported these events during multiple sessions,” if accurate.

“Client was trafficked.”

Write: “Client reported that another person kept her passport, controlled her movement, required her to work without pay, and threatened harm if she left.”

“Client should get asylum.”

Write: “Client reported fear of returning to her country and was referred for immigration legal screening.”

What to include by pathway

Possible VAWA-related documentation

Relationship to abuser, marriage/family status, shared residence, reported abuse, threats of deportation, document/financial control, isolation, emotional/physical/sexual abuse, stalking, threats involving children, safety planning.

Do not write: “Client qualifies for VAWA.”

Possible U visa-related documentation

Type of crime reported, police/prosecutor/court/CPS involvement, victim-witness contact, report numbers, restraining orders, injuries, emotional harm, fear of participating, threats/intimidation after reporting.

Do not write: “Client qualifies for a U visa.”

Possible T visa-related documentation

Document control, debt bondage, forced labor, commercial sex exploitation, threats, isolation, control of movement/housing/transportation, withholding wages, threats to family, fear of law enforcement, inability to leave, physical/emotional harm.

Do not write: “Client was legally trafficked.”

Possible asylum-related documentation

Fear of return to country of origin, past harm, threats, family/gang-related harm, domestic/sexual violence abroad, LGBTQ-related harm, political/religious/ethnic harm, failure of authorities to protect, trauma symptoms tied to return, deadlines.

Do not write: “Client has asylum.”

Possible SIJ-related documentation

Age, parent-related abuse/neglect/abandonment, family separation, unsafe reunification, parent absence, caregiver relationship, school/mental health impact, child safety concerns, court involvement, custody/guardianship needs, urgency due to age.

Do not write: “Child qualifies for SIJ.”

Quick Mini-Templates

Mini-template for referral notes

Client is being referred with consent for immigration legal screening. Client reports [general issue]. Client reports immigration-related safety concerns including [threats of deportation / document control / fear of police or court / fear of return / trafficking indicators / pending immigration court / other]. Client’s safe contact method is [safe contact]. Client prefers [language]. Please avoid [unsafe contact method]. Client has [urgent deadline, if any].

Mini-template for safe contact note

Client requests safe contact only by [phone/text/email/other] at [time]. Client states that [voicemail/mail/email/patient portal/text] is not safe. Client requests that messages avoid the words [domestic violence/immigration/legal/shelter/police/court/other]. Client states that [person] may monitor [phone/email/mail/location/social media].

Mini-template for service confirmation

This letter confirms that [Client Name] received services from [Organization] beginning on or around [date]. Services included [general services]. The client reported safety concerns related to [general issue]. Our organization provided [support/referrals]. This letter is not a legal opinion and does not determine eligibility for any immigration benefit.

Final Reminder

The best support letters are not the longest letters. They are the safest, clearest, most accurate letters.

The strongest documentation question is:
“Am I documenting useful facts within my role, while protecting the survivor’s safety and avoiding legal conclusions?”

Confidentiality & Safety

Deep dive into technology safety, safe contact protocols, and information-sharing limits.

Purpose of this page

Confidentiality is not just a paperwork rule. It is a safety tool.

For survivors of domestic violence, sexual assault, stalking, trafficking, coercive control, and immigration-related abuse, the wrong message, document, address, voicemail, email, portal notification, or referral note can create danger. The goal is not to make every provider a privacy lawyer or cybersecurity expert. The goal is to help every partner pause before sharing, sending, storing, printing, or discussing survivor information.

Core Principle

Ask before sharing. Confirm before contacting. Limit what you disclose.

The safest question is:
“Could this communication, document, or disclosure put the survivor at risk if the wrong person saw it?”

Why confidentiality matters in immigration work

Immigrant survivors may face overlapping risks. An abuser, trafficker, employer, sponsor, landlord, family member, or community member may use immigration information as a weapon. They may threaten to:

  • Call immigration or ICE.
  • Withdraw a petition.
  • Hide documents or destroy mail.
  • Monitor phones & track location.
  • Expose the survivor’s address.
  • Contact family abroad.
  • Report the survivor to police, court, or child welfare.
  • Interfere with legal appointments.

A survivor may avoid help not because they do not want support, but because every system feels searchable, reportable, or dangerous.

What information may be sensitive

Personally identifying information is broader than people think. It is not only a person’s name. A combination of details can identify someone, especially in a small community. Treat the following as potentially sensitive:

  • Name, address, phone.
  • Email, date of birth.
  • Immigration status.
  • A-number, USCIS receipts.
  • Immigration court dates.
  • ICE check-ins.
  • Safe address / shelter.
  • Workplace / schools.
  • Medical / therapy records.
  • Police / court orders.
  • Photos of injuries.
  • Text messages.
  • Social media screenshots.
  • Passport, ID, work permit.
  • Information about sexual assault or trafficking.
  • Information about LGBTQ identity.
  • Pregnancy details.
  • Family abroad info.
  • Cooperation with law enforcement.
  • Fear of return to country.

When in doubt, minimize details.

Consent & Information Sharing

Coordination is important, but coordination does not automatically mean open sharing. Before sharing survivor information, confirm that consent is voluntary, informed, specific, time-limited, and documented.

Release-of-information checklist

  • The release is written and the survivor understands it.
  • The release names the person or agency receiving information.
  • The release describes what information may be shared and the purpose of sharing.
  • The release has an expiration date or reasonable time limit.
  • The release is not a blanket permission for everything.
  • The survivor knows they can limit the scope and revoke permission.
  • The release does not authorize unsafe disclosure of addresses, shelter location, children’s information, or immigration details unless truly necessary.

Safe Contact Protocols

Before calling, texting, emailing, mailing, or sending a referral, ask:

  • What is the safest way to contact you?
  • Is it safe to leave a voicemail or text?
  • Does anyone else access your phone, know your passwords, or check your email?
  • Does anyone track your location, open your mail, or monitor your social media?
  • Does anyone receive appointment reminders or have access to family-sharing settings?
  • What time of day is safest? What words should we avoid in messages?
  • Should we use a neutral caller ID, subject line, or message?

Voicemail safety

Before leaving voicemail, ask whether it is safe. If safe, keep it neutral: “Hi, this is [Name]. I’m calling to follow up. Please call me back at [number] when you can.” Do not mention domestic violence, immigration, VAWA, U visa, shelter, etc.

Email & Portal safety

Email can be unsafe if the abuser has access to recovery tools or shared devices. Patient portals can expose appointment reminders and billing statements. Always use neutral subject lines like “Follow-up” or “Meeting confirmation.”

Technology safety red flags

Technology may be part of the abuse. Listen for:

  • “He always knows where I am.”
  • “She reads my messages.”
  • “He installed something on my phone.”
  • “She controls the phone plan.”
  • “He tracks my car.”
  • “She has my passwords.”
  • “He gets alerts when I leave.”
  • “She uses cameras in the home.”

A survivor should not be told to immediately turn everything off without a safety plan. Sudden changes can alert an abuser and escalate danger. Consider connecting them with a technology-safety advocate and planning changes carefully.

Immigration-specific confidentiality concerns

Immigration information can be especially dangerous. Do not casually share immigration status, pending applications, A-numbers, USCIS receipt numbers, prior removal orders, ICE check-ins, or court dates. A survivor may be harmed if an abuser learns they filed or are considering filing for VAWA, U visa, T visa, asylum, or SIJ.

Safe address in immigration referrals: Never assume the address on a document is safe. Ask if the abuser lives there, checks mail, or uses "informed delivery" emails. Secure a safe mailing address (like an attorney or agency) immediately.

Internal information sharing & Documentation

Even within the same organization, survivor information should be limited to staff who need it for service delivery. Case notes should be accurate, role-limited, and safe.

Safer wording for case notes:

  • “Client reports that partner monitors phone and requested text contact only between 9 AM and 12 PM.”
  • “Client reports that mail to home address is unsafe.”
  • “Client consented to referral for immigration legal screening.”

Avoid:

  • “Client is illegal.”
  • “Client refuses to cooperate.”
  • “Client is hiding from immigration.”
  • “Client only wants papers.”

Helpful Scripts

Technology safety script

“Before we contact you or send anything, I want to check what is safe. Some people have partners, family members, employers, or others who monitor phones, email, mail, location, or online accounts. What is the safest way for us to reach you?”

Consent script

“Before I share anything with another organization, I want to explain what I would share, why, and who would receive it. You can say yes, no, or limit what gets shared. You do not have to agree to a referral to receive support here.”

Referral script

“Some of what you shared may be important for immigration legal screening. I cannot tell you whether you qualify, but I can help connect you to someone qualified. We can share only the minimum information needed, and we can decide together what contact method is safe.”

Key Takeaway

Confidentiality is survivor safety. Every partner should slow down before sharing, sending, storing, printing, or discussing survivor information.

The strongest confidentiality question is:
“Does this person know exactly what I am about to share, with whom, why, for how long, and through what method — and could that sharing put them at risk?”
